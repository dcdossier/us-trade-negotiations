Understanding Data and Law

What are SPS measures?

Published 14 Aug 2025 Updated 17 Aug 2025

understanding a key negotiation issue of Japan and India

SPS or Sanitary and phytosanitary measures are health and safety measures that are adopted by countries to ensure the safety in the quality of imports that come from other countries. These are aimed at preventing health hazard for humans, plants or animals from an external invasive element that cause food-borne diseases, pests, and related illnesses.

WTO’s Agreement on the Application of Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures sets a few common rules and guidelines on these measures which also extend further from country-to-country depending on the environment and native preferences.

In the 2025 trade negotiations these measures were cited as an ‘unfair’ trade barrier by the US while negotiating with Japan and India.

Japan’s stated how their SPS restrictions were necessary against American Apples which has the risk of introducing fire blight bacterial infection to Japan’s larger fruit farming industry. America rejected these risk-claims to be unscientific and unjustified on Japan’s part citing a 2003 WTO ruling to justify their counter. These claims have been extended to Japan’s larger non-tariff barriers through high quality standard requirements for primary sector imports such as diary, fruit, and beef (through Bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE) or Mad Cow disease) among others. America claims that Japan cites unscientific and unjustified claims to protect its domestic markets from external competition in the primary sector.

India’s case has SPS’s focused both on disease prevention and also partially socio-cultural sentiments. Focused on the primary sector areas of diary, poultry, pork and seeds more primarily, America argues against the ‘unscientific nature’ of India’s SPS measures. In the case of poultry, pork and eggs, India aims to avoid all possible sources of low pathogenic avian influenza (LPAI) leading to stringent regulatory mechanisms. India also has a strong diary regulatory standards requiring diary products to be free of pesticides, heavy metal residues, drugs, or antibiotics. In the case of the source of these dairy products, India has a certification clause that requires the animals that are not fed animal derived byproducts like whey, or slaughterhouse wastes like bones, blood or processes meat and bone. These regulations rooted in Indian culture are criticised as being unscientific and unjustified by the US citing a 2015 WTO ruling supporting the same. US also argues how the rules are more stringent for imports than domestic producers citing biases and protectionism.

SPS measures thus forms a core focus on US tariff negotiations with Japan and India where US openly criticises Japan’s regulatory standards and India’s import certification mechanisms that work not as a protection against diseases but help protect and unfairly advantage domestic industries.
