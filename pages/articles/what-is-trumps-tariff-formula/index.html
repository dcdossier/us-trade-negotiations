Understanding Data and Law

What is Trump’s Tariff formula?

Published 16 Aug 2025 Updated 17 Aug 2025

An explainer on the factors determining these tariff

What are Tariffs and how are they determined?

Tariffs are defined as taxes imposed by a government on goods and services imported from other countries. These are determined by several economic, social, and political factors including the classification of a good, and its country of origin.

Domestic consideration for import tariff calculations are often determined by strong considerations for availability of an item domestically, its cost of production, its tentative impact on Consumer Price Index (and Inflation). It also depends on the economic principle of comparative advantage and consider its impact on the existing multilateral global supply chains.

Global factors for import tariffs include the classification of a good under coding systems (such as the Harmonised System or HS code) which categorises products accordingly. They are also determined by assessed value of an item through its transactional value, insurance and freight among others. They are also determined by WTO, previous multilateral or bilateral agreement with the aim of ensuring simplicity and transparency in the process to provide clarity to the businesses and consumers. Tariffs are also put in place in relation with the existing taxes, duties, quotas and other non-tariff barriers, to maximise or restrict its trade impact.

Often times Tariffs are use to:

  1. Protect domestic indutries
  2. Balance trade
  3. Reduce external dependency on certain items
  4. Act as a leverage in trade negotiations
  5. Raise government revenue

Why is the US waging a Tariff war?

For the United States however, trade deficits and extensive imports have worked to ensure the dominance of the US dollar as the ‘reserve currency’ of the world. However, over the last few decades, this is also resulting in a decline of American manufacturing sector, which are moving to more economically viable countries like China with cheap labour and lower regulatory standards. It is argued that this has led to massive unemployment in the US. Additionally, American businesses have also grown frustrated with a lack of access to the domestic markets of other countries with their strict tariffs and non-tariff barriers. This was viewed as a greater injustice considering minimal level of tariffs or non-tariff barriers in the US for imports from many countries. In the context of this growing political demand that the Donald Trump administration developed a tariff agenda to counter the ‘unfair’ trade practices of its trade partners, leading to the April 2 ‘Liberation Day’ tariffs.

Trump’s Tariff formula

Trump’s formula of reciprocal tariffs targeting countries with whom the US has a trade deficit took the U.S. trade deficit with a specific country, divided it by total imports from that country, and then halved the result to determine the tariff rate.

Eg:

U.S. imports from China = $440 Bn
US trade deficit with China = $295 Bn
Tariff Rate = (295/440 * 100) / 2 = 33.5

The formula was based on US Census trade data for imports and exports with each country. The White House and USTR used this formula to calculate tariffs for each country individually, rather than matching foreign tariffs on U.S. goods. The calculation did not directly factor in foreign tariffs, currency manipulation allegations, agricultural restrictions, or non-tariff barriers, although Trump publicly cited those issues as justifications. Experts have also criticised this formula stating how it is not ‘reciprocal’ in anyway as it does not consider the tariffs other countries place on the US.

Before the Trump administration’s tariff announcements the tariff calculation process in the US was carried out by the US congress using Harmonised Tariff Schedule (HTSUS). These took into consideration agreements and negotiated rates coupled with legal restrictions and regulations such as anti-dumping mechanisms.

The Trump administration’s introduction of a formulaic approach to tariff determination, thus, has been widely criticised not only for lacking a sound analytical basis but also for facing legal challenges on the grounds that it exceeds the President’s authority by infringing upon Congressional authority.
