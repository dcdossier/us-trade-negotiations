What is EU’s Anti-Coercion Instrument

Published 7 Aug 2025 Updated 17 Aug 2025

A brief explainer of EU’s trade ‘Bazooka’

The European Union’s Anti-Coercion Instrument (ACI) is a strategic trade defence tool designed to deter and respond to economic coercion by non-EU countries in 2023. Economic coercion refers to actions by a third country that intentionally pressure the EU or a Member State to change a policy decision, often by threatening or imposing trade restrictions like tariffs or potentially sanctions. The ACI enables the EU to respond through retaliatory tariffs, import/export (I/E) restrictions, intellectual property (IP) restrictions, foreign direct investment (FDI) controls, and market access limitations — including in public procurement, food safety, chemicals, and pharmaceuticals.

The ACI can be triggered in scenarios where the EU faces unjustified sanctions or threats against its regulatory autonomy — for example, if a foreign government blocks exports to punish the EU for environmental legislation or diplomatic actions. Importantly, the ACI does not require the EU to prove WTO illegality — only coercive intent.

In a latest CNBC report, it was stated how a tariff of more than 15% by the United States would likely trigger a ACI use. If US were the target, the ACI’s use could escalate into a serious transatlantic trade conflict, potentially disrupting high-value trade in sectors like tech, pharma, and energy. It could also signal a shift in EU foreign policy toward strategic autonomy, complicating cooperation in NATO, climate policy, and global technology governance.

Use Scenarios for ACI

The ACI is designed for situations where:

  • A non-EU country uses trade threats or restrictions to pressure the EU or its Member States.
  • Example: If the U.S. were to penalize EU companies over digital taxes or environmental standards.
  • Example: China’s informal trade embargo against Lithuania after Taiwan-related diplomatic decisions — a real-world case that helped motivate the ACI.

The Commission investigates the coercive act and determines the intent, not just the effect. The EU can act even if coercion is only threatened, not yet materialised.

Here is a step-by-step guide to how the EU Anti-Coercion Instrument (ACI) can be used and implemented, based on the official legal regulation and policy documents:

How is ACI implemented:

a step-by-step guide

Step 1: Detection of Economic Coercion

  • The process begins when a third country (non-EU) applies or threatens to apply measures intended to pressure the EU or a Member State to change a policy decision.
  • Coercion could include:
    • Export/import bans
    • Selective customs enforcement
    • Public procurement boycotts
    • Investment blockages
    • Threats of retaliation for diplomatic or regulatory decisions (e.g., environmental or digital laws)

Step 2: Commission Assessment

  • The European Commission assesses whether the measure constitutes “economic coercion.”
  • Criteria considered:
    • Is there an intent to influence the EU or a Member State?
    • Is the pressure targeting sovereign policy decisions?
    • Is the measure unjustified or disproportionate?

Note: The Commission does not need to prove WTO illegality, only coercive intent.

Step 3: Diplomatic Engagement

  • Before taking action, the EU must engage in dialogue and negotiations with the third country.
  • The goal is to de-escalate the situation without resorting to countermeasures.
  • This step may involve:
    • Diplomatic notes
    • Bilateral talks
    • Mediation or third-party involvement

Step 4: Proposal of Countermeasures

If dialogue fails, the Commission proposes a response to the Council of the EU, which could include one or more retaliatory countermeasures:

Step 5: Council Approval

  • The Council of the European Union must approve the proposed countermeasures by qualified majority.
  • This avoids a full unanimity requirement, enabling faster action.

Step 6: Implementation of Measures

  • Once approved, the European Commission implements the measures through delegated regulations or executive acts.
  • The measures are legally binding and immediately applicable across the EU.

Step 7: Monitoring and Adjustment

  • The Commission monitors:
    • The impact of EU countermeasures
    • The third country’s response
  • It can adjust, suspend, or revoke the measures if:
    • The coercion ends
    • An agreement is reached
    • EU interests require a change in strategy

Estimated Timeline

  • Investigation and diplomacy phase: Weeks to months
  • Council approval and implementation: Can be relatively quick (few weeks) once coercion is confirmed
  • Total process: Typically within 6 months, but flexible depending on the context
