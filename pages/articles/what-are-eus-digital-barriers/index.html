Understanding Data and Law

What are EU’s Digital barriers?

Published 17 Aug 2025 Updated 28 Aug 2025

Explainer on EU’s Digital taxes and laws and their impact on US trade negotiations.

Digital services and goods form a large portion of US-EU trade. Of the total EU-US trade in services, a striking 77.2% are digital including intellectual property, digital business services, cloud computing, among others. In terms of value, trade in services between the EU and US reached €816.9 Bn in 2024, nearly equaling their trade in goods (€867.1 billion). The EU imports more than €300 billion in digital services from the US annually, highlighting the immense role of American tech and digital firms in Europe’s marketplace. It is this high volume of trade that made Digital services and its associated laws and taxes, a key point of negotiations between US and EU. Additionally, Donald Trump’s personal and political affiliation with digital and social media organisations also makes this a important industry of interest for the administration.

EU’s Digital barriers

The EU’s strict digital barriers are motivated by several factors ranging from a rise of US-Digital tech giants in European markets and EU’s ambition for Digital sovereignty. EU aims to tax these giants for the high market access European nations provide to them while also ensuring strong data privacy for their citizens. As a push to implement these goals, EU has introduced several digital taxes and regulations regulating social media spaces and AI products.

  • Digital Services Tax (DST):
    It is designed to ensure that large multinational digital companies (often U.S.-based such as Meta, Google, Amazon) pay taxes proportionate to their European revenues, since many have historically paid less due to the digital nature of their services. These DSTs typically targets online advertising, digital intermediation, and user data sales with taxes vary by country generally ranging from 1.5% to 7.5%, hitting firms above specific revenue thresholds. In the course of the US trade negotiations however, several countries have paused or scaled back their plans for wider DST implementation.
  • Digital Services Act (DSA):
    In effect since August 2023, now enforcing strict content moderation, user protection, and transparency measures for major online platforms (including U.S. companies). Starting July 1, 2025, all adult users in the EU must verify their age via a special app to access social media and streaming platforms (YouTube, TikTok, Snapchat, X, etc.). Non-verified users may lose access, and violations can lead to fines up to 6% of global turnover. Through this, the aim is preventing underage access to platforms, strengthening parental controls, privacy-by-default for minors, and enabling investigations into compliance by platforms like Meta, TikTok, and adult entertainment sites. This model also motivated Canada to implement the same only to rescind its decision during the course of the negotiations with the US.
  • EU AI Act:
    The EU AI Act is the first comprehensive law regulating AI use in the EU, aiming to ensure safety, transparency, accountability, and protection of fundamental rights. Regulated by the European Commission and the EU AI office, this law divides AI use in three categories and regulates the use of AI for specific tasks which are to be assessed before market placement. These categories are
  • Unacceptable-risk AI: Fully ban on AI systems that are considered a clear threat to safety, livelihoods, or rights. Eg: Social scoring or remote biometrics.
  • High-risk AI: Stringent compliance (documentation, audits, transparency and risk mitigation) applied to AI systems deployed in sensitive areas where errors can significantly affect health, safety, or fundamental rights.
  • Limited-risk AI: Transparency and AI content warning required for AI systems with a more moderate impact on users.
  • Minimal-risk AI: General legal obligations required for AI applications with very little potential for societal harm.

Major rules for general-purpose AI systems kicked in August 2, 2025: transparency, technical documentation, and disclosure of copyrighted materials for model training. Extra rules apply to high-risk AI models.
