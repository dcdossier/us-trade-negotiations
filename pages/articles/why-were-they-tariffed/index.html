Understanding Data and Law

Why were they tariffed?

Published 17 Aug 2025 Updated 17 Aug 2025

Understanding the logic behind some of highest tariffed nations with some of the lowest trade volumes with the US.

In 2025, US Tariff rates reach an historic high with an average rate of 17.7%. Although most countries became subjects to these tariffs, some major players such as the USMCA countries, EU countries, China, India, UK among a few others were subjected to above average tariff rates. These rates went beyond the 17.7% average and were largely justified by the trade volume they had with the US as in the case of China and USMCA, their political significance to the Trump administration as in the case of Mexico and China or US’ lack of access to large domestic markets as in the case of India. There were other countries that lacked these factors of volume and market and were excepted.

However, there is also a peculiar list of countries with a low trade volume, comparatively low political significance and even low population that became targets of above average tariffs. These countries are Switzerland, Taiwan, Myanmar, Libya, Algeria, Tunisia, Moldova, Bosnia and Herzegovina, and Serbia.

In 2024, Switzerland had about $188 billion in overall trade with the US, making up roughly 3.5% of total US trade. Taiwan followed closely with $185.7 billion, also representing about 3.4% of US trade. Myanmar’s trade with the US reached approximately $730 million, or just 0.013%. Libya’s figure was around $2 billion (0.037%), while Algeria recorded $3.9 billion (0.07%). Trade between the US and Tunisia amounted to about $2 billion, also 0.037% of the total. Moldova’s trade figure was near $90 million (0.0017%), Bosnia and Herzegovina saw $320 million (0.006%), and Serbia had $1.4 billion (0.026%). With the exception of Switzerland and Taiwan, all these countries each account for less than one-tenth of one percent of the United States’ total international trade volume, highlighting their limited role in US global trade for 2024. Then why were these countries tariffed?

1. Formula and ‘Reciprocity’

The answer to this lies in Trump’s trade formula– which was a numerical formula that took into consideration the trade deficit and imports from a country. Applying these even to countries with low trade volume and trade opportunity for the US was a political display of ‘equal treatment‘ for all nations- friend, foe, partner or adversary alike.

2. Risk of Transshipment

Trump administration argues how America’s average MFN (most-favoured nation) tariff is among the lowest in the world (~3.3%), while many other economies—even those with small trade volumes—maintain higher tariffs on US goods. Additionally, tariff policy often applies universally or to broad categories of countries to plug legal loopholes. Thus even if these countries aren’t major trade players, low or zero tariffs for them might create transshipment opportunities for countries like China to evade tariffs.

3. Symbolism and signalling

The administrations also aims to use tariffs—even on minor trade partners—as political or bargaining signals and displaying the US as a strong, ruthless power fighting for its national interest- America first.

4. Sector protection

Some small or low-population countries like Bangladesh, Serbia, Myanmar, Cambodia are significant exporters in certain sectors like textiles or footwear. US tariffs target these sectors for protection or to address alleged unfair trade practices or labor standards.

For countries like Switzerland or Taiwan, specific issues such as taxation of certain products (gold, pharmaceuticals, advanced electronics), perceived currency manipulation, or even political positioning (Taiwan’s ambiguous status) can also be factors that lead to tariffs, despite small population or modest overall trade.

Even for small countries or those with little direct leverage, US tariff policy is driven more by reciprocity, sectoral protection, and enforcement of broad trade principles than by sheer trade volume or partner population. This is to prevent arbitrary openings for trade diversion, reinforce negotiation stances, and protect specific industries, not just to maximise tariff revenue from high-volume partners or gain market access.
