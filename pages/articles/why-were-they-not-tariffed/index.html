Understanding Data and Law

Why were they not tariffed?

Published 17 Aug 2025 Updated 17 Aug 2025

Understanding the countries who escaped reciprocal tariffs.

Belarus, Burkina Faso, Cuba, North Korea, Palau, Russia, Seychelles, Somalia and Vatican City made it to a short list of countries that were not a subject to Trump’s reciprocal tariffs on ‘Liberation Day’, April 2, 2025. Canada and Mexico were also excluded from these reciprocal tariffs but due to the USMCA.

For the other countries on this list however, two factors resulted in this outcome- Trade volume with the US and Political reasons. Here’s why Belarus, Burkina Faso, Cuba, North Korea, Palau, Russia, Seychelles, Somalia, and Vatican City were excluded from U.S. reciprocal tariffs, along with an overview of U.S. trade with these countries before 2025.

1. Low Trade volume and unimportant sectors

US imports from Belarus totaled $21.2M, mainly tools, metal products, milling products, seafood, textiles, books, and dairy while exports were minimal, only several million dollars annually, trending downward. Similarly, US imports from Burkina Faso were $4.7M, mainly edible fruits/nuts, vehicles, machine parts, essential oils, antiques with limited exports of agricultural and industrial machinery. For the tiny Island of Palau too this trend continued with US exports of $17.5M (food, frozen meat, broadcasting equipment) and an import of $506K consisting of live fish and shells. For Seychelles as well, the US imports $2.75M (mainly fish, pharmaceuticals, boats, electronic equipment) while the US exports are slightly higher, mainly donated supplies and small machinery. This low trade volume in relatively unimportant sectors kept them out of Trump’s tariff list.

2. Socio-Cultural and Geopolitical Reasons

There are also countries who were off the list for primarily political reasons. Cuba and North Korea have almost all U.S. trade forbidden by embargo and sanctions laws. There’s no significant legal trade, so reciprocal tariffs become irrelevant. Russia, one of the major US trade partners before the Ukraine War, is another important exclusion. US-Russia saw dramatic decline of trade since 2022 following the war resulting from heavy sanctions thus making tariffs little to no impact. Palau’s U.S. Compact of Free Association also plays a political role in keeping it away from the tariff list allowing it US economic aid and support in exchange for military rights. Similarly, for Vatican City, whose trade is minimal and is restricted essentially to cultural or ceremonial items, reciprocal tariffs serve no practical function.

Somalia too is a unique mix of both the scenarios with a US export market of nearly $7M and an import of $124K of primary sector or minor manufacturing items. Additionally, Somalia’s internal instability and lack of development made it an unlikely target to be hit by tariffs.

The listed countries have been excluded from reciprocal tariffs because trade volumes are trivial, existing sanctions already ban most trade, or special diplomatic arrangements/policy goals make tariffs unwarranted. The U.S. instead focuses on national security, humanitarian, or diplomatic considerations for these nations, making tariff retaliation moot.
